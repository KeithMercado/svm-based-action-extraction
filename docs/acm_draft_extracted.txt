ABSTRACT 
The rapid shift toward hybrid work environments has intensified the challenge of "information overload," particularly in gene rating 
accurate Minutes of the Meeting (MoM) for multilingual dialogues. In the Philippine context, the prevalence of code-switching (Taglish) 
in professional and academic settings presents a significant barrier for standard Natural Language Processing (NLP) models, which often 
lack the transparency required for critical task tracking. This study presents a high -precision action item extraction system utilizing a 
Linear Support Vector Machine (SVM) framework. To address the linguistic complexity of Filipino -English discourse, we developed a 
massive, diverse corpus consisting of 180,000 samples spanning business, healthcare, education, and legal domains. 
Our methodology employs Feature Hashing for 210 vector mapping and a Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) training approach to ensure 
scalability and model interpretability. Experimental results demonstrate that the linear decision function effectively identi fies linguistic 
markers unique to Taglish impera tives (e.g., "paki-send" or "need to finish" ), achieving robust F1 -scores across all four domains. A 
comparative analysis reveals that increasing the data scale from 10k to 180k significantly stabilizes precision, providing a transparent 
alternative to "black-box" deep learning models. This research  concludes that a well -optimized Linear SVM, paired with a large -scale 
multilingual dataset, offers an efficient and interpretable solution for automated meeting documentation in code-switching environments.

1 INTRODUCTION 
The rapid transition to hybrid and remote work environments has led to a significant increase in the volume of digital 
meetings conducted across various sectors, including business, academia, and healthcare. While video conferencing tools 
provide accessibility, they also generate a massive amount of unstructured conversational data. Distilling these long-form 

2 
transcripts into concise, actionable "Minutes of the Meeting" (MoM) remains a labor-intensive task that is prone to human 
error and information loss. 
A critical component of effective meeting summarization is Action-Item Detection—the process of identifying specific 
tasks, assignments, or commitments made during a discussion. However, automating this process presents two primary 
challenges. First, existing Natural Language Processing (NLP) models often struggle with "Taglish," a common linguistic 
phenomenon in the Philippines where English and Tagalog are used interchangeably. Second, many modern "black -box" 
architectures, such as deep neural networks, lack the interpretability required for users to understand the logic behind task 
extraction. 
This study addresses these challenges by proposing an interpretable action-item detection system using a Linear Support 
Vector Machine (SVM). By leveraging Linguistic Feature Modeling (LFM), the system identifies imperative constructs 
and domain-specific markers without the heavy computational requirements of transformer -based models. To ensure the 
model’s robustness across diverse professional fields, we utilize an expanded corpus of over 180,000 samples, covering 
specialized sectors such as Legal, Engineering, and Healthcare. This research aims to provide a high-precision, transparent, 
and scalable framework for automated task management in linguistically diverse environments. 
1.1 Background of Study 
In professional and organizational settings, meetings serve as a central mechanism for communication, collaboration, and 
decision-making. They are critical for assigning tasks, discussing project directions, and resolving issues. However, long 
and unstructured meeting transcripts—particularly in hybrid and remote work environments—pose challenges in tracking 
important information, especially action items. These action items, which often represent assigned tasks or decisions 
requiring follow -up, are frequent ly buried within lengthy dialogues, making them difficult to identify and retrieve 
efficiently without automated support. 
To address this, the field of natural language processing (NLP) has explored various techniques for meeting 
summarization and action -item detection. Many early systems have focused on abstractive or extractive summarization, 
aiming to condense key points of a meeting transcript. However, summarization alone does not suffice when the goal is to 
isolate action-oriented utterances. Thus, action-item detection has emerged as a specialized task within the broader field of 
dialogue understanding. 
Recent advancements in NLP, particularly transformer -based models such as BERT and its derivatives, have 
significantly improved performance in text classification and summarization tasks. For example, Golia and Kalita (2024) 
employed a hybrid extractive-abstractive summarization pipeline using BERTScore to identify action points in meetings 
with high accuracy. However, these models are computationally intensive, less interpretable, and often require fine-tuning 
with large, domain -specific datasets —limitations that make them less practical for low -resource environments or for 
deployment in real-time systems. 
Further, the work by Kiminos and Daskalaki (2020) compared Naïve Bayes and SVMs for classifying useful 
information in business meetings and found that SVMs offered better precision for decision and task -related utterances, 
particularly when feature enginee ring emphasized domain -relevant keywords, modal verbs, and syntactic patterns 
(Kiminos & Daskalaki, 2020 ). This affirms the potential of combining domain -specific linguistic features with classical 
models to develop efficient, interpretable, and scalable systems for action -item detection. Moreover, the IEEE study by 
Xie et al. (2023) on regularized context m odeling provided further support for leveraging structured context and lexical 
semantics in classical ML pipelines, improving generalizability across meetings with varied formats (Xie et al., 2023).

Building upon this, the current study seeks to apply Linear SVM modeling integrated with engineered linguistic features 
to detect action items in long meeting transcripts. Rather than relying solely on deep contextual embeddings, this approach 
emphasizes syntactic and semantic cues found in real human dialogue, such as imperative phrasing, modals (e.g., “should”, 
“need to”), named entity references, and discourse markers. This not only enhances transparency and interpretability but 
also allows for greater control over model behavior in culturally nuanced scenarios. 
The study further emphasizes local adaptation by integrating the AMI Meeting Corpus, a well-established multilingual 
meeting dataset, and the i -TANONG dataset, which includes authentic Philippine -based meeting dialogues in English, 
Tagalog, and Cebuano. This combination enables the system to generalize well across varied linguistic environments while 
ensuring relevance to hybrid work settings in the Philippines —where code-switching, politeness strategies, and indirect 
speech are more prevalent. 
By focusing on action -item detection rather than full summarization, this research directly targets usability and 
workflow integration in task management systems, project collaboration tools, and productivity software used by Filipino 
professionals. It also lays the groundwork for extending such systems to other regional languages and settings, promoting 
inclusivity in NLP applications. 
1.2 Statement of the Problem 
In the shift toward remote and hybrid work, organizations have become heavily reliant on digital meetings. While this 
ensures continuity, it has resulted in an overwhelming number of long -form transcripts that are difficult to manage. Most 
actionable tasks and decisions are buried within hours of dialogue, leading to “information overload” and missed deadlines. 
Existing Natural Language Processing (NLP) solutions often struggle with the informal nature of spoken conversation and 
the high computational cost of transformer models. Furthermore, most systems are optimized for Western discourse, failing 
to capture the unique code-switching and politeness strategies used by Filipino professionals. Specifically, this study will 
seek to answer the following questions: 
1. How will the integration of linguistic feature modeling (e.g., modal verbs, dependency relations, and discourse 
markers) improve the detection accuracy of action items in long meeting transcripts compared to baseline models? 
2. What is the benefit of using a Linear SVM model over deep learning "black -box" architectures in terms of model 
interpretability for researchers and meeting participants? 
3. How effective is the system in a localized context, specifically in identifying action items that involve Taglish 
(Tagalog-English) code-switching and Filipino-specific politeness markers (e.g., "paki," "po")? 
4. How efficient is the proposed Action-Item Detection System in terms of: 
a. Precision and Recall: Its ability to correctly identify tasks without flagging general conversation. 
b. Interpretability: The clarity of "why" a sentence was classified as an action item. 
c. Generalizability: Its performance across different datasets (AMI vs. i-TANONG). 
d. Contextual Clarity: The readability of the final summarized action items.

2 SCOPE AND LIMITATIONS 
The primary focus of this research is the development of an interpretable action -item detection system and a minutes -of-
the-meeting generator tailored for long, unstructured meeting transcripts using a Linear Support Vector Machine (SVM). 
The system utilizes Linguistic Feature Modeling, targeting imperative verbs, modals, and discourse markers, to identify 
actionable tasks within long transcripts. Training and evaluation are conducted using the AMI Meeting Corpus and the i-
TANONG dataset, specifically covering English and Tagalog within the Philippine hybrid work context. The final output 
is a structured Minutes of the Meeting (MoM) report containing extracted tasks, assignees, and deadlines. 
Conversely, certain boundaries and constraints are recognized in this study. The research is delimited to text -based 
analysis; thus, it does not include the development of a Speech -to-Text (STT) engine and relies on the assumption that 
input transcripts are of sufficient quality. Furthermore, the system focuses on extractive summarization techniques for the 
generation of meeting minutes, meaning it will not perform abstractive summarization or paraphrase dialogues into new 
text. In terms of linguistic cover age, while the study includes major Philippine languages, it does not account for other 
regional dialects or highly specialized technical jargon outside the scope of the provided datasets. Methodologically, as the 
study utilizes a Linear SVM rather than deep learning architectures like Transformers, the system may face limitations in 
capturing long-range contextual dependencies that span across distant parts of a long transcript. Finally, the system is 
designed for asynchronous processing of completed transcripts rather than real-time, live-streamed action-item detection

3  RELATED WORK 
The automated analysis of meeting transcripts has seen significant growth with the rise of remote collaboration. This 
section reviews the evolution of action -item detection and summarization, specifically focusing on the transition from 
traditional linguis tic models to machine learning approaches, and the unique challenges posed by multilingual 
environments. 
3.1 Automatic Text Summarization 
Automatic text summarization (ATS) aims to distill documents into shorter versions while keeping the key information 
and maintaining the same meaning. Critical reviews of the current state of ATS research identify two main approaches to 
summarization, extractive (taking the salient sentences) and abstractive (generate summaries that paraphrase). Widyassari 
et al. (2022) provide a review of over 85 methods for ATS from 2008-2019. The authors conclude there's solid performance 
on very short texts; however, many of those methods were unable to maintain contextual coherence or factual consistencies 
with longer, conversational data. 
A broader view of summarization methodologies is offered by Yadav et al. (2022), who conducted a systematic meta -
analysis on both extractive and abstractive summarization techniques. Their study categorizes various models based on 
preprocessing strategies, text representations, and evaluation metrics. Extractive methods, which select existing sentences 
from a source text, are noted for their efficiency, while abstractive techniques generate novel phrases and require deeper 
semantic understanding.  
Patel and Pareek (2021) provide a comprehensive overview of the evolution, advancements, and remaining challenges 
in automatic text summarization, with particular emphasis on deep learning –based methods. Their study charts the 
transition from statistical and rule-based systems to neural networks, such as sequence-to-sequence models and transformer 
architectures. A key issue identified is factual consistency, wherein abstractive models may hallucinate or generate 
incorrect content. Scalability and generalization across domains also remain pressing concerns.  
Finally, Zhang et al. (2020) reconceptualize extractive summarization as a text matching task, where candidate 
sentences are aligned against a semantic representation of the source document. By reframing the summarization process 
in this retrieval-like structure, their approach leverages neural matching architectures that improve the selection of relevant 
and salient sentences. This shift toward sentence-level matching helps mitigate common limitations of traditional extractive 
systems, such as inclusion of non-essential or off-topic content. In the context of meetings, where actionable insights are 
often dispersed across varied dialogue turns, such matching -based strategies could enhance the focus and precision of 
automated summaries. 
3.2 Action-Item Detection with Feature Engineering & SVM 
Action-item detection has become an essential task in automatic meeting analysis, as it allows systems to identify and 
extract actionable outcomes from conversational transcripts. Stojanovic et al. (2023) introduced an approach that leverages 
regularized context modeling to detect meeting action items from transcripts, emphasizing the importance of contextual 
features such as nearby dialogue acts and sentence types. Their model integrates handcrafted features with Support Vector 
Machines (SVMs), showing tha t the inclusion of regularized context windows significantly boosts the performance of 
traditional machine learning models. 
Gishamer et al. (2023) explored action item detection within multiparty conversational speech by analyzing meeting 
transcripts through a system that integrates domain -specific features. The researchers emphasized the challenges of 

6 
overlapping speech, ambiguous action phrasing, and varied speaker intents, and addressed them through an enriched feature 
set and fine-tuned SVM decision boundaries.  
Kiminos et al. (2020) conducted a comparative study between Naïve Bayes and Support Vector Machines for 
classifying decisions in business meeting transcripts. Although both models aimed at identifying meaningful segments 
such as decisions and tasks, SVM demonstrated superior performance due to its ability to model high-dimensional feature 
spaces and handle sparse data. Their feature engineering pipeline included term frequency -inverse document frequency 
(TF-IDF), cue phrases, and syntactic structures, which significantly improved the precision of action item detection.  
In broader research related to conversational structure recognition, Li et al. (2021) examined the role of punctuation, 
labels, and context in helping transformers and other models identify dialogue acts. While their focus was not directly on 
SVMs, their findings supported the underlying premise that contextual and structural features are critical for understanding 
speaker intent. Their conclusions offer indirect validation for the feature engineering strategies employed in SVM -based 
models. 
3.3 Linguistic and Contextual Features 
Understanding the role of linguistic features in Natural Language Processing (NLP) tasks has proven critical, especially in 
applications like meeting summarization and action -item detection. One pivotal study by Tenney et al. (2019) explored 
how contextual word representations—such as those derived from models like BERT —can effectively capture various 
layers of linguistic knowledge including syntax, semantics, and coreference resolution. Their findings demonstrated that 
deep language models inherently learn  transferable linguistic patterns that benefit downstream tasks such as sentence 
classification, entailment detection, and question answering. In the context of meeting transcripts, where utterances are 
often incomplete or informal, leveraging such context ual representations can help identify nuanced linguistic cues that 
distinguish actionable statements from general dialogue. 
Further expanding on linguistic intricacies, the work of Crossley et al. (2020) provided a comprehensive overview of 
how linguistic features contribute to writing quality and textual coherence. While their research focused on writing 
development, their fra mework of analyzing lexical diversity, syntactic complexity, and cohesion can also be adapted to 
spoken dialogue in meetings. Transcripts often exhibit varying levels of coherence and structure depending on the speaker 
and context. Recognizing and modeling  such features can significantly enhance the identification of task -oriented 
utterances or action items, especially when differentiating between vague suggestions and clearly defined directives. 
On the other hand, contextual modeling combined with syntactic analysis has also been applied in sentiment analysis, 
which shares structural similarities with action -item detection. In their study, Wu et al. (2020) proposed a hybrid model 
that integrated contextual embeddings with syntactic features to improve aspect-based sentiment analysis. Their approach 
emphasized the importance of understanding the relationship between sentence elements to derive specific insights. In a 
similar vein, action-item detection can benefit from this method by focusing not just on isolated keywords but also on how 
clauses are structured—identifying intents, subjects, and corresponding actions from nested or compound sentences often 
found in meetings. 
Lastly, the recent work by Yadav et al. (2024) introduced a dialogue -act–enhanced framework for meeting 
summarization, showcasing the power of combining extractive and abstractive techniques through role- and context-aware 
processing. By classifying utterances based on dialogue acts (e.g., question, suggestion, decision), their model improved 
the quality of summaries, particularly in capturing meeting intent and actionable content. This approach aligns closely with 
the goals of action -item detection in long  meeting transcripts, as understanding the speaker’s intent and the surrounding 
context is crucial in distinguishing routine conversational exchanges from those that require follow-up or delegation.

3.4 Meeting Transcript & Corpora 
The use of structured meeting transcripts plays an important role in understanding communication flow, speaker intent, 
and decision-making cues in professional environments. In the work by Zhong et al. (2021), the authors proposed a context-
aware summarization model that segments meetings using a sliding window approach to maintain coherence and topic 
continuity. This approach enhances extractive summarization by capturing dynamic shifts in conversation, which is 
essential in real-life meeting scenarios where speakers often digress or loop back to earlier points. This study underlines 
the need for adaptive segmentation methods to improve downstream tasks like action -item detection and dialogue 
understanding—key elements in our proposed study. 
Further, supervised machine learning approaches have been proven effective for textual tasks, especially when paired 
with annotated corpora. Kowsari et al. (2019) presented a comprehensive survey on supervised machine learning 
techniques for automatic text  classification, emphasizing the role of high -quality training datasets and linguistic feature 
modeling in improving classification outcomes. Their review of classifiers, including support vector machines (SVMs), 
highlighted how context -sensitive learning combined with structured corpora can lead to robust text classifiers. This 
reinforces the decision to use Linear SVM in our system, where learning from annotated corpora like AMI and i-TANONG 
is vital for detecting and classifying actionable content in transcripts. 
Meanwhile, recognizing the diversity in conversational behavior and local context has become increasingly relevant in 
corpus development. Blodgett et al. (2022) argued for using conversational corpora that capture nuances in speech, such 
as turn-taking, politeness strategies, and cultural expressions. They highlight how traditional NLP datasets often overlook 
the diversity of language use across communities. By leveraging culturally aware corpora like i -TANONG, which reflect 
Filipino discourse styles, our s ystem aims to model and detect action items more accurately in meetings grounded in 
Philippine contexts—ensuring relevance, usability, and inclusivity. 
Lastly, the MeetScript project by Ma et al. (2023) introduced innovative transcript-based interfaces that facilitate better 
engagement in group video meetings. Their system capitalized on real -time transcript interactions, allowing users to 
highlight, annotate, and navigate content seamlessly during meetings. This approach demonstrates the increasing relevance 
of intelligent transcript interfaces for hybrid work environments. It also supports the motivation behind our study: to not 
only detect action items but to potentially feed them into usable systems that enhance meeting productivity through 
intelligent summaries and task tracking based on localized and labeled corpora. 
3.5 Sequence Labeling and Dialogue Act Recognition 
Dialogue act recognition plays a critical role in understanding the structure and intent behind spoken utterances in meetings. 
It facilitates the identification of who spoke, when, and with what purpose—elements that are fundamental to action-item 
detection and meeting summarization. Recent advancements in natural language processing have focused on enhancing 
sequence labeling techniques to improve the recognition of speaker intent and conversational structure. 
Kim et al. (2024) introduced Dual Process Masking, a cutting -edge approach to dialogue act recognition that 
independently learns from both utterance -level and contextual features, resulting in higher precision in complex 
conversational settings. Similarly,  Chapuis et al. (2020) proposed a hierarchical pre -training strategy that captures 
dependencies at both the sentence and dialogue levels, improving classification performance in spoken dialog 
environments. 
Raheja and Tetreault (2019) further advanced the field by developing a context-aware self-attention mechanism, which 
dynamically allocates attention based on surrounding dialogue turns, enabling more accurate recognition of speaker intent.

Meanwhile, Żelasko et al. (2021) highlighted the critical role of context, punctuation, and dialogue labels in helping 
transformer models effectively model conversational flow. 
These contributions emphasize the value of incorporating conversational features such as speaker turns, discourse 
signals, and temporal sequencing —elements that are also leveraged in this study’s SVM -based action -item extraction 
framework. 
3.6 SVM-Based Techniques and Classical ML Models 
Although deep learning dominates much of modern NLP, classical machine learning techniques —particularly Support 
Vector Machines (SVM)—remain highly relevant, especially in applications requiring interpretability, efficient training, 
and feature engineering. Classical models offer computational advantages and often perform competitively in structured 
domains like meeting analysis. 
Abualigah et al. (2019) provided a foundational review of text summarization techniques, noting that while deep 
learning enables abstraction, classical methods excel in extraction -based tasks when paired with linguistic and positional 
features. Kumar et al. (2020) applied this hybrid approach in healthcare, demonstrating that ensembling classical and deep 
models can improve outcomes in tasks such as morbidity detection from clinical notes. 
Kiminos and Gkaris (2020) explored the application of Naïve Bayes and SVM models in identifying decision points 
within business meeting transcripts. Their findings confirmed SVM’s ability to detect structured, task -oriented segments 
within dialogue—a key r equirement in action -item detection. Furthermore, Kadhim (2019) presented a comprehensive 
review of supervised learning approaches for text classification, reinforcing the robustness and adaptability of SVM in 
various NLP contexts. 
Building upon these works, this study employs an SVM model enriched with linguistically -informed features, such as 
modal and imperative verbs, part -of-speech (POS) patterns, dependency relations, and sentence positioning, to classify 
action items within segmented meeting data. The result is a lightweight yet interpretable classifier suitable for real-time or 
resource-constrained environments. 
3.7 Post-processing & Action-Item Extraction Techniques 
Post-processing is essential for transforming raw predictions into coherent, human -readable outputs. In meeting 
summarization, this step ensures that extracted action items are properly contextualized and seamlessly integrated into the 
final summary. This contributes to usability by clarifying vague or incomplete utterances. 
Pandya and Gawande (2022) developed an end -to-end system for the automatic generation of meeting minutes, 
emphasizing structured layout and clear delineation of decisions and action items. Vapnik and Izmailov (2021) introduced 
a Reinforced SVM model with m emorization mechanisms, enabling models to retain and leverage prior conversational 
context—an essential capability in dialogues where action items are often distributed across multiple turns. 
Sadia et al. (2025) extended these techniques into multilingual domains by applying large language models to action -
item detection in Urdu meeting transcripts. Their work validates the versatility of hybrid models in diverse linguistic 
contexts. Similarly, Sachdeva et al. (2021) investigated pre -trained transformers for corporate meeting analysis, showing 
significant improvements in performance due to the incorporation of contextual embeddings. 
Informed by these developments, this study introduces a contextual rephrasing mechanism that uses a fine-tuned BART 
model to re -summarize action items along with their surrounding sentences —defined as a "neighborhood." This step 
improves clarity and resolv es pronouns or incomplete references, ensuring that each action item is self -contained, 
actionable, and aligned with the surrounding dialogue context.

3.8 Multimodal & Cultural Adaptation in Summarization 
Multimodal aspects have confirmed improvements for automatic meeting summarization, since a multimodal automatic 
summarization system considers a variety of content, especially by looking at audio and video to determine the content's 
significance. For example, Nihei and Nakano (2019) designed a multimodal utterance-predictive model by identifying both 
verbal and nonverbal features (e.g., voice intensity, location of a speaker's gaze) to improve salient-sentence detection (F1 
of 0.827). Their best performing  deep-learning model outperformed models created by hand coding, showing meeting 
summarization model quality can be improved through multimodal studies. 
From the results on cultural aspects of automatic summarization, training models for local languages and style is 
important for accessibility. An example of a language model with a cultural connotation is the iTANONG -DS dataset 
(Visperas et al., 2023), which includes labeled corpora of the Philippine languages Tagalog and Cebuano in NLP tasks like 
summarization. The iTANONG -DS dataset allows a developer or test researcher to train and evaluate models that 
understand syntactic structures of Philippine langua ges. As you mentioned, the use of code -mixing as an authentic local 
expression can assist with localized summarization performance for users in the videos. 
Together, these findings provide research justification to pursue the multimodal design and localization to the Philippine 
context. By integrating audio/text features for action-item detection and language embeddings (fine-tuning or no hashing), 
the framework will be able to provide adequate summaries that are not just accurate, but in context with localization in 
mind for users in the meetings/contexts.

5 METHODOLOGY & FRAMEWORK 
This chapter details the systematic six-phase methodology used to develop an interpretable system for action-item detection 
and meeting summarization. The design focuses on transforming meeting transcripts into formal Minutes of the Meeting 
(MoM) report by  combining Linear SVM for precise task extraction with BART -based abstractive summarization. By 
bridging the gap between human dialogue and professional documentation, this pipeline ensures that the final output is 
both computationally accurate and operationally useful for academic and professional institutions. 
5.1 Data Acquisition & Preprocessing 
This phase focuses on assembling and preparing a high-quality, culturally relevant dataset to train the action-item detection 
model. Two primary sources are utilized: the AMI Meeting Corpus, which contains English transcripts from real -world 
team meetings with speaker turns, roles, and dialogue annotations; and the iTANONG dataset, a Taglish (Tagalog-English) 
open-domain dialogue dataset. The AMI dataset provides structured meeting-style discourse essential for training general 
action-item classifiers, whil e the iTANONG dataset brings in cultural and linguistic diversity reflective of Filipino 
communication patterns. Sentences from both datasets are manually annotated as either “Action Item” or “Not an Action 
Item” using custom guidelines. For the iTANONG dataset in particular, the focus is on identifying indirect task delegation, 

11 
politeness markers (e.g., “po,” “baka pwede”), and volunteer phrases (e.g., “sige, ako na lang”). Each annotated sentence 
is enriched with metadata such as speaker role, sentence position within the turn, use of honorifics, and Taglish detection. 
The resulting structured dataset combines cross -cultural examples and supports the training of a Linear SVM model that 
generalizes across both formal and culturally grounded communication, making the system adaptable for Philippine 
academic and organizational use.

5.2 Topic Segmentation 
Long meeting transcripts are difficult to summarize holistically due to their length and varied content. This phase divides 
transcripts into smaller, coherent topic -based chunks using topic segmentation algorithms adapted from Golia and Kalita 
(2023). Three segmentation strategies are considered: Chunked Linear, Simple Cosine Similarity, and Complex Cosine 
Similarity, all leveraging MPNet embeddings to compute semantic similarity between speaker turns. By segmenting the 
transcript into manageable units, the system ensures that subsequent action item detection and summarization tasks remain 
context-aware and computationally feasible. This segmentation also preserves conversational flow and thematic continuity, 
enabling more accurate extraction of action-relevant content.

5.3 Extractive Action Item & Opinion Classification using Linear SVM 
At the core of the thesis is the detection of action items using a Linear Support Vector Machine (SVM) classifier enhanced 
by linguistically engineered features. For each sentence within a topic chunk, a set of lexical, syntactic, speaker-based, and 
culturally grounded features are extracted. These features include the presence of action verbs, modal auxiliaries, task -
deadline phrases, honorifics, and indicators of Filipino-style politeness and indirect delegation. The Linear SVM is trained 
on the manually annotated dataset from Phase 1 to distinguish between action items and non-action items. This interpretable 
machine learning approach allows for transparency in classification decisions and supports the cultural localization of the 
model. Identified action  items are then contextualized using their neighboring sentences, preparing them for integration 
into the final summary.

5.4 Abstractive Summarization 
This phase focuses on generating readable and informative summaries that not only condense the transcript content but 
also integrate detected action items. A fine -tuned BART (Bidirectional and Auto -Regressive Transformer) model, pre -
trained on conversational summarization datasets such as SAMSUM and XSUM, is used to produce general summaries 
for each topic-based chunk. Once action items are identified and contextualized, they are appended to the chunk summary, 
and the combined text is passed through the BAR T model again to ensure coherence and fluency. This dual -stage 
summarization ensures that actionable content is not only preserved but also highlighted within a natural -language 
summary, resulting in summaries that are more meaningful, task-oriented, and usable.

5.5 Recursive Merging & Minutes of the Minutes (MoM) Construction 
To create a unified record for the entire meeting, this phase merges all topic -level summaries into a structured, 
comprehensive document. If the combined output remains within the token limits of the summarization model (e.g., 
BART’s 1024-token input limit), it is processed through a final refinement layer to improve cohesion. Once the content is 
within this limit, the system automatically generates a formal Minutes of the Meeting (MoM) output. This structured final 
document organizes the refined text into professional sections, including meeting metadata, discussion highlights, and a 
consolidated list of the action items detected in Phase 3. If the initial combined text is too large, a recursive summarization 
strategy is employed—treating the concatenated summaries as a new document and reapplying the process until the content 
is condensed enough to be formatted into the final MoM. This ensures the final output is not just a summary, but a 
professional-grade record that is concise, coherent, and operationally useful. 
5.6 Evaluation & Metrics 
To validate system performance and demonstrate its real-world usability, this phase includes both quantitative evaluation 
and model explainability. The action-item classifier is assessed using metrics such as accuracy, precision, recall, and F1 -
score, while the quality of the summaries is measured using ROUGE and BERTScore. In addition, the interpretability of 
the Linear SVM model is highlighted by analyzing feature importance weights—demonstrating which linguistic or cultural 
features most influence action-item detection. This level of transparency fosters trust in the system and ensures its outputs 
are verifiable and aligned with human expectations, especially in culturally nuanced contexts like Filipino meetings. 
5.7 THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK 
The theoretical framework of this study centers on the belief that effectively identifying action items in lengthy transcripts 
requires a thoughtful balance between advanced AI and traditional, transparent machine learning. By rooting the system 
in Linguistic Feature Modeling, the research suggests that tasks aren’t just random words but are signaled by specific 
human patterns, such as the use of "need to," "should," or direct command verbs. To ensure this process isn't just a "black 
box," the framework uti lizes Explainable AI through a Linear SVM, allowing us to see exactly which words or markers 
triggered a decision. This is especially vital for Cultural Adaptation, as it accounts for the unique way Filipinos delegate 
tasks through Taglish and politeness m arkers like "paki" or "po." Finally, the framework employs Recursive Processing 
and Contextual Rephrasing to transform these identified items into a structured Minutes of the Meeting (MoM), ensuring 
that "buried" tasks are extracted along with their responsible parties and deadlines into a clear, actionable format.

7 CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
This study successfully demonstrated the viability of an interpretable, scalable framework for action-item detection within 
multilingual (Taglish) meeting transcripts. By prioritizing linguistic feature modeling and a Linear SVM over high -
resource deep lea rning, the research provides a computationally efficient and transparent solution for administrative 
automation in the Philippine context. 
7.1 Summary of Findings 
The experimental results validate the effectiveness of the Linear Support Vector Machine (SVM) when trained on diverse 
datasets totaling over 180,000 samples. Key findings include: 
• High Recall Performance: Across all datasets, including Formal English (0.99) and Expanded Contexts (0.98), the 
model demonstrated exceptional recall. This ensures that the system captured nearly all actionable commitments, 
a critical requirement for reliable Minutes of the Meeting (MoM) generation. 
• Linguistic Resilience:  The model maintained a robust F1 -score of 0.89 to 0.90 in meeting -specific and 
comprehensive contexts. While accuracy naturally dipped to 73.43% in heavily mixed Taglish sets, the model 
remained effective at identifying tasks despite non-standard syntax. 
• Scalability through Incremental Learning: The implementation of SGDClassifier and Feature Hashing allowed 
for the processing of massive data scales without specialized GPU hardware. This proves that high -performing 
NLP tools can be deployed on standard office hardware. 
• Cultural Interpretability: Feature importance analysis confirmed that Filipino-specific markers, such as the prefix 
"paki-" and code-switched verbs like "i-check," are the primary drivers of classification. This transparency allows 
users to verify the "logic" behind every flagged t ask, distinguishing this approach from "black -box" neural 
networks. 
7.2 Future Work 
Based on the performance metrics and observations during development, the following areas are recommended for future 
research: 
• Live Transcription Integration: Future iterations should transition from asynchronous file processing to Real-time 
Speech-to-Text (STT) integration. This would enable the system to provide immediate notifications to 
participants as tasks are assigned during a live session. 
• Hybrid Semantic Modeling: To bridge the accuracy gap in complex Taglish dialogue, researchers may explore a 
hybrid architecture. A Linear SVM can be used for high -speed keyword extraction, while a lightweight 
transformer like DistilBERT could resolve ambiguous or "indirect" tasks that lack explicit imperative markers. 

17 
• Expansion of Regional Dialects:  Given the success of the Taglish model, the framework should be expanded to 
include other major Philippine languages, such as Cebuano and Ilocano, ensuring the tool’s utility across the 
entire archipelago. 
• Multi-modal Sentiment Analysis: Incorporating prosodic features (tone of voice) could help the model distinguish 
between a casual suggestion and a formal directive, further refining the precision of the extraction process.
